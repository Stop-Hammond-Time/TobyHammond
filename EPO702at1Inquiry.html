Curriculum Content

The following tasks involve viewing artwork. These are meant as an engagement activity to the inquiry and followed up with class discussion that develops inquiry questions (the list from section 1 can give ideas if needed) and inspires children to engage in the further research and responding activities that follow. Listening to student input is important here as the teacher facilitates the students developing new understandings of the world (Jenkins, 2015). In doing so, it is often important for the educator to relinquish control over the conversation, and step into a facilitating role (Dinham & Chalk, 2017). Similarly, it is important to discuss artwork that children have made, allowing time for educators and students to ask questions about the art making process, stimulating reflection on the thoughts, feelings, observations and experiences embodied within (Dinham & Chalk, 2017).

The pollution affecting Indigenous rock art at Murujuga

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3-8r47as63Y

This youtube video is an informative investigative piece on the effects of air pollution on traditional indigenous rock art of the Ngarluma people in Karratha, Western Australia. The video is incredibly short for the value it provides in access to a vast amount of curriculum. Through interviews with credible people, it explores the artwork and its importance to the Ngarluma people. It also discusses the source of the air pollution, its general effect globally and in this specific circumstance.

Students then express their thoughts, emotions and opinions through creating their own rock art, emphasising originality and personal expression. While students may take stylistic inspiration from indigenous rock art, it is important to remind students that these are sacred artworks for indigenous people, and to use symbols of spiritual significance without a full appreciation for their meaning is cultural appropriation.

LO1 Discuss the significance of the Ngarluma people’s rock art, and the damaging effect that air pollution is having on it.

LO2 Discuss the people who spoke in the video and how their credentials (the Ngarluma man’s membership in his community and the scientist’s field of study and academic credentials) add credibility to what they say and distinguish fact from opinion. Discuss the points of view of these people, and of the people involved with the industry creating the pollution, brainstorming questions for further investigation.

LO3 Create rock artwork that expresses personal ideas, feelings and observations on air pollution. Then present to and discuss with the class

Responding to other artworks such as “The great delusion” and my video from section 2 could also be used as an artwork to view and respond to. The inclusion of artwork with a message running counter to that of the inquiry promotes critical consumption of art and information, as well as differentiating fact from opinion. (ACHASSI077)

Artwork, such as my video highlights how individuals, including children, have the power to make choices and changes that make a difference in the issue of air pollution. It is an exciting, real-world application of their learning, and many children love riding bikes.

Towards the end of the inquiry, the students’ learning culminates in the organisation of an event or action that they plan, advertise and educate about through art. Drawing inspiration from the artworks, students research (use the following links, WHO WHO and Clean Air Fund) and discuss the reputability of the sources and why this is important), and create an ‘air pollution’ themed event such as “ride your bike to school day” or a walking school bus. They then create posters (digitally or hand crafted), videos or presentations that promote the event emphasising educating their audience on air pollution and sustainable practices. The educative side of this is important for the audience (who will mostly be students of the school) as Gilbert, Tudball and Brett (2015) highlight “These special days need prior learning and subsequent follow-up so that students understand the significance of the issue being addressed… and the solution proposed” (p. 468). Ideally, this would be more effective if taken as part of a whole school approach, preferably all in an inquiry such as this one.
