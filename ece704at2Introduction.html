Introduction

Why Pattern

Patterns serve as the cohesive element in this portfolio. Recognizing, replicating, and extending patterns are skills linked to mathematical success and early algebraic thinking, making pattern recognition a pivotal concept in early education (Gifford, 2019; MacDonald, 2018). Young learners naturally grasp patterns observed in daily life, facilitating their application in creatively engaging with mathematics (MacDonald, 2018).

The importance of creativity in mathematics extends beyond fostering a positive learning attitude; it deepens conceptual understanding and nurtures creative competencies often overlooked in STEM fields, yet essential for thriving amidst swift technological advancements (Ashbrook, 2016; Björklund & Ahlskog-Björkman, 2017; Shen & Edwards, 2017; Bergen, 2009; Marsh and Young, 2015).

Rhythmic notation - patterns in and through music

The rhythmic notation experience offers learning opportunities both in and through music, emphasising genuine musical education while highlighting the mathematical elements in children’s creative works, as advocated by Björklund & Ahlskog-Björkman (2017). Engaging videos serve as an effective rhythm-teaching tool, captivating students with their entertaining nature.

Patterns through Creative and Body-based Learning

Conversely, the Drama/Dance experience utilises dance and drama as tools through which students learn patterns. Drawing inspiration from Paige et al. (2021), who discuss science education via drama, I’ve adapted their pedagogical strategies to teach mathematics through an amalgamation of drama and dance, or ‘creative and body-based learning’ (CBL) (p. 75). This approach is further enriched by the picture book “Pattern Fish” (Harris, 2000).

Children’s literature proves to be a formidable tool for mathematics instruction, as aligning stories with student interests markedly boosts engagement—a leverage point for enhancing the teaching process (Dunphy, 2020). “Pattern Fish” exemplifies this by showcasing a broad spectrum of repeating patterns, contributing to the foundational education in patterns.

These pedagogical experiences are tailored for the Cycle 1 students (ages 3-6) at the Montessori school where I teach.
